5. Data Collection and Analysis Module

Collecting and analyzing assessment data is essential for understanding student achievement of learning outcomes and identifying opportunities for program improvement. This module guides you through planning and implementing effective data collection and analysis processes.

Data Management Considerations:

  • Storage location: Where will assessment data be stored?
  • Access permissions: Who will have access to the data?
  • Anonymization: How will student identities be protected?
  • Retention period: How long will data be kept?
  • Documentation: How will collection methods be documented?

Step 2: Ensuring Data Quality

Data Quality Checklist:

Strategies for Improving Data Quality:

  • Calibration sessions where multiple evaluators assess the same work samples
  • Pilot testing assessment instruments before full implementation
  • Clear documentation of data collection procedures
  • Training for all individuals involved in assessment
  • Multiple measures for each outcome to triangulate findings

Step 3: Analyzing Assessment Data

Quantitative Analysis Approaches:

  • Descriptive statistics: Calculate means, medians, ranges, and standard deviations
  • Performance distributions: Determine percentage of students at each performance level
  • Comparative analysis: Compare results across cohorts, sections, or time periods
  • Correlation analysis: Examine relationships between different outcomes or factors
  • Disaggregation: Analyze results by relevant student characteristics

Qualitative Analysis Approaches:

  • Thematic analysis: Identify patterns and themes in narrative data
  • Content analysis: Systematically categorize and quantify qualitative information
  • Case studies: Examine individual student experiences in depth
  • Narrative analysis: Analyze stories and reflections for meaning
  • Artifact analysis: Examine student work products for evidence of learning

Step 4: Interpreting Results

Interpretation Framework:

  1. Describe the results: What patterns or trends do you observe in the data?
  2. Compare to expectations: How do results compare to program standards or targets?
  3. Consider context: What factors might have influenced these results?
  4. Identify strengths: Where are students performing particularly well?
  5. Identify areas for improvement: Where are students struggling or underperforming?
  6. Generate hypotheses: What might explain the observed patterns?
  7. Consider implications: What do these results suggest for program improvement?

Example Interpretation (Psychology Research Methods Outcome):

Results Description:

Analysis of research proposals from the Research Methods course shows that 78% of students met or exceeded expectations for designing appropriate methodology, but only 45% met expectations for identifying limitations of their chosen methods.

Comparison to Expectations:

The program target is 75% of students meeting expectations for all components of research design. While methodology selection exceeds this target, recognition of limitations falls significantly below expectations.

Contextual Factors:

The Research Methods course was recently revised to emphasize practical application, which may have improved methodology selection. However, critical evaluation of methods receives limited attention in the curriculum.

Strengths:

Students demonstrate strong ability to match appropriate methods to research questions and design procedurally sound studies.

Areas for Improvement:

Students struggle to critically evaluate methodological limitations and consider alternative approaches.

Hypotheses:
  1. The curriculum may emphasize procedural knowledge over critical evaluation.
  2. Students may lack exposure to methodological critiques in the literature.
  3. Faculty may focus feedback on technical correctness rather than limitations.
Implications:
  1. Incorporate more examples of methodological critiques throughout the curriculum.
  2. Develop specific assignments focused on identifying limitations.
  3. Revise rubrics to place greater emphasis on critical evaluation.

Step 5: Setting Performance Targets

Types of Performance Targets:

Absolute targets:

Specific percentage of students achieving a performance level

Example: "85% of students will score 'proficient' or higher on the capstone rubric"

Improvement targets:

Increase in performance compared to previous assessment

Example: "Average scores will improve by 0.5 points on the 4-point communication rubric"

Comparative targets:

Performance relative to peer institutions or external standards

Example: "Student performance will meet or exceed the national average on standardized exams"

Process targets:

Completion of assessment activities or improvement initiatives

Example: "All core courses will implement common rubrics for assessing written communication"

Target Setting Guidelines:

  • Base targets on program expectations, disciplinary standards, and institutional priorities
  • Consider historical performance data when available
  • Set challenging but achievable targets
  • Review and adjust targets periodically based on results and changing circumstances
  • Differentiate between minimum acceptable performance and aspirational goals
