2. Learning Outcomes Development Module

Learning outcomes are specific, measurable statements that describe what students should know, value, or be able to do upon completion of your program. This module guides you through the process of developing effective learning outcomes that align with your program's mission and institutional priorities.

Step 1: Understanding Learning Outcomes

What Makes an Effective Learning Outcome?

Effective learning outcomes are:

  1. Student-centered - Focus on what students will learn or be able to do, not what will be taught
  2. Measurable - Can be assessed through direct observation of student performance or work
  3. Clear and specific - Use precise language that all stakeholders can understand
  4. Achievable - Realistic given program resources and student preparation
  5. Relevant - Connected to program goals and meaningful to stakeholders
  6. Appropriately challenging - Match the expected level of the degree program

Step 3: Drafting Program Learning Outcomes

Learning Outcome Formula:

Upon completion of this program, students will be able to [ACTION VERB] [SPECIFIC KNOWLEDGE, SKILL, OR VALUE] [OPTIONAL: BY WHAT MEANS OR TO WHAT LEVEL].

Examples by Discipline

+
Biology:
  • Upon completion of this program, students will be able to design and conduct experiments to test biological hypotheses using appropriate controls and statistical analyses.
  • Upon completion of this program, students will be able to analyze biological systems at multiple levels of organization, from molecules to ecosystems.
Business Administration:
  • Upon completion of this program, students will be able to evaluate business problems using quantitative and qualitative methods to support decision-making.
  • Upon completion of this program, students will be able to develop strategic plans that address organizational challenges in a global context.
Education:
  • Upon completion of this program, students will be able to design inclusive learning environments that address diverse student needs and promote educational equity.
  • Upon completion of this program, students will be able to assess student learning using multiple methods and use results to improve instruction.
Engineering:
  • Upon completion of this program, students will be able to design solutions to complex engineering problems that meet specified needs with consideration of public health, safety, and welfare.
  • Upon completion of this program, students will be able to communicate technical information effectively to both technical and non-technical audiences.

Step 4: Outcome Review and Refinement

Outcome Quality Checklist:

Common Pitfalls to Avoid:

  • Using vague verbs like "understand," "know," or "appreciate"
  • Including multiple outcomes in a single statement
  • Focusing on course activities rather than student learning
  • Setting expectations too low or too high for the degree level
  • Creating outcomes that cannot be meaningfully assessed

Step 5: Organizing Program Learning Outcomes

Most programs benefit from having 5-8 program learning outcomes that comprehensively address key competencies while remaining manageable for assessment.

Categorization Options:

  • By knowledge domain (e.g., theoretical knowledge, methodological knowledge)
  • By skill type (e.g., technical skills, communication skills, analytical skills)
  • By program component (e.g., core curriculum, specialization, capstone)
  • By professional standards (e.g., aligned with disciplinary or accreditation standards)

Example Organization (Psychology Program):

Knowledge Outcomes:
  1. Apply psychological theories and concepts to explain human behavior and mental processes.
  2. Evaluate the scientific merit of psychological research using methodological principles.
Skill Outcomes:
  1. Design and conduct basic psychological research using appropriate methods and ethical principles.
  2. Analyze quantitative and qualitative data using appropriate statistical techniques.
Professional Application Outcomes:
  1. Communicate psychological concepts effectively in written and oral formats.
  2. Apply psychological principles to address real-world problems in diverse contexts.
